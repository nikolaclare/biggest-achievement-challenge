Conquering the Great Victorian Rail Trail

A Solo Journey of Gravel Cycling and Bikepacking

Explore My Journey

The Journey Begins

Cycling has always been a passion, but last year, I needed more than just another ride. Having just completed my graduate certificate in data science, I was at a crossroads. Transitioning out of nursing, after the emotional toll of working through the trauma of COVID, left me feeling lost. I could no longer continue in a career I had once loved. At one of my lowest points, I turned to nature as a way to reconnect and rediscover joy. That’s when I decided to push myself with my longest ride yet—over 240 km across four days on the Great Victorian Rail Trail.

The ride wasn’t just about the physical challenge; it was a journey to heal and find peace. Each section of the trail presented its own beauty and obstacles, giving me moments to reflect, recharge, and test my limits. Every kilometre was a step toward rediscovering my passion and finding new strength.

The Journey: Day-By-Day

Day 1: Tallarook to Molesworth - 55km

The excitement of starting the journey, securing all my gear to the bike for the first time, and pushing through the first day of gravel and trails...

The adventure began in Tallarook, a quaint town known for its rich history and scenic landscapes. The first leg of the journey led through the picturesque Trawool Valley, where the terrain was relatively flat, making for a smooth start. As I pedaled along, stunning views of rolling hills and the meandering Goulburn River accompanied me.

A highlight of the day was passing through the historic Cheviot Tunnel, Victoria's longest rail trail tunnel. Eventually, I arrived in the quiet town of Molesworth. Nothing was open, and not a soul was in sight. Thankfully, the accommodation had left out a few slices of bread and Vegemite—just enough to keep me going after a long day on the bike.

Day 2: Molesworth to Mansfield - 66km

A long stretch with some challenging climbs and beautiful views along the way...

Day two took me from Molesworth to Mansfield, covering about 66 kilometers. The terrain became more demanding with gentle climbs, but the stunning scenery made up for the effort. The landscape transitioned from lush farmland to more rugged terrain, offering views of distant mountain ranges.

Art installations dotted the trail, celebrating the region’s culture and history, and the trail crossed several small streams, allowing for refreshing breaks. The day’s most memorable moment was an aggressive magpie that dive-bombed me twice, leaving a mark on my new helmet! After dodging a large snake with the help of a wandering echidna, I finally reached Mansfield. Though sore and exhausted, a shower, massage, and hearty pub meal rejuvenated me, leaving me ready for the next leg of the journey.

Day 3: Mansfield to Yea - 83km

The toughest day, but also the most rewarding, as I pushed through the longest distance yet...

Day three stretched 83 kilometers from Mansfield back to Yea, the most challenging day so far. The trail offered spectacular views of valleys and mountains, but the effort required for the climbs tested my endurance. A long, exhilarating downhill section gave me a much-needed break and a sense of reward for all the effort.

The day wasn’t without its surprises. I encountered a farmer who had blocked the trail to Alexandra, forcing me to retrace my steps. With dusk approaching, I had to divert directly to Yea, adding more kilometers to an already lengthy ride. The detour was tough, but the feeling of accomplishment when I rolled into Yea that night made it all worth it.

Day 4: Yea to Tallarook - 39km

The final stretch of the adventure, with a mix of relief and nostalgia setting in...

The last leg of my journey, 39 kilometers from Yea back to Tallarook, felt both familiar and reflective. As I retraced my path, I took in the familiar sights with a sense of accomplishment. The route was mostly flat, with a few gentle gradients to keep me on my toes, but the knowledge that I was nearing the end gave me extra energy.

Along the way, I made sure to revisit some favorite spots—scenic overlooks and quiet rest stops that had been highlights on the first day. Each stop felt like a chance to savor the experience before it ended. By the time I rolled into Tallarook, there was a deep sense of satisfaction. Completing the loop, back where it all began, felt like the perfect conclusion to an unforgettable journey.

Highlight Moments

Ready to Ride

As I geared up for my first multi-day bikepacking trip along the Great Victorian Rail Trail, excitement buzzed in the air. I carefully loaded my rear bag and handlebar bags, distributing the weight evenly to maintain balance. Essentials like plenty of water were a must, as long stretches of the trail had no access to refills. I packed layers of clothing, knowing the weather would range from warm spring days to the cooler conditions near the base of the snowfields in Mansfield. Along with snacks to keep me fueled, I also included tools for minor repairs and a first-aid kit for peace of mind, especially given the trails more isolated stretches with no mobile reception. With my bike fully prepped, I was ready to embrace the adventure that lay ahead.

Trail Art

The Great Victorian Rail Trail (GVRT) isn’t just a cyclist’s paradise filled with breathtaking landscapes and serene trails—it's also a journey into the heart of art and culture. Scattered along the 134-kilometer stretch are over two dozen art installations that add a unique, creative layer to the ride. The trail becomes a living gallery, inviting us to pause and reflect on these connections while pedaling through the scenic beauty of Taungurung Country. The art installations highlight the region’s rich Indigenous heritage and make the experience even more meaningful. These artworks transform the GVRT into more than just a ride—it’s an artistic adventure that leaves you with a deeper appreciation of both the landscape and the stories it holds.

Wildlife Encounters

Throughout the journey, I had some memorable wildlife encounters—both awe-inspiring and slightly terrifying! I spotted kangaroos hopping through the bush, a quintessential Australian sight. But the real drama came courtesy of a particularly aggressive magpie, which dive-bombed me on both passes, even managing to take a chunk out of my brand-new helmet! Just when I thought the excitement was over, an echidna wandered across the path, saving me from riding straight over a large snake basking in the sun. It was nature at its wildest, and it certainly kept me on my toes!
